## Medium (Facebook / Instagram — 100–200 words)

**Option A — human story:**

Your neighbour might be paying less for the same groceries — and neither of you would know it.

Surveillance pricing is when stores use your personal data (where you live, when you shop, your purchase history) to figure out the highest price you'll pay, and charge you that. It's not a market price. It's your price.

The people it hits hardest are the ones who can least afford it — shoppers with predictable routines, parents of young kids, people in urgent situations. Your necessity is their pricing signal.

The NDP is calling on the federal government to ban it — in-store and online. Manitoba's NDP government has already introduced a provincial bill. We need federal action now.

Sign the petition → ndp.ca/ban-surveillance-pricing

**Option B — loyalty card:**

When you scan your loyalty card, you're earning points worth a few cents. The store is earning a detailed map of your shopping habits, your health needs, and your vulnerabilities — and feeding it to a pricing algorithm.

Surveillance pricing uses that data to charge you more than someone with a different profile. It's unfair, and it's spreading into groceries, rental housing, ride-sharing, medicine, and more.

The NDP wants to ban it. Add your name → ndp.ca/ban-surveillance-pricing

## Longer (Facebook thread or page post — 300–400 words)

**Title: "How stores are using your data to charge you more — and what we're doing about it"**

Imagine two people walking into the same grocery store, putting the same box of cereal in their baskets. One pays $4.99. The other pays $5.79. They have no idea.

That's surveillance pricing — and it's already here.

Retailers and tech companies collect enormous amounts of data about you: your loyalty card scans, your location, your browsing history, your purchase patterns. An AI algorithm processes all of it to estimate what you personally are willing to pay — and sets your price accordingly.

The people it targets hardest aren't the ones who can afford it. If you're a low-income shopper with a predictable routine — same store, same time, same items — you're actually easier to profile. Consistency reads as captivity to the algorithm. If you're a parent searching for medicine for a sick child at 2am, the algorithm sees the urgency and prices accordingly. If you just lost someone and need to book a last-minute flight, that grief registers as inflexibility — and the price goes up.

Your loyalty card points are worth cents. The data you hand over is worth far more — to them.

There's another wrinkle: when multiple grocery chains use the same third-party pricing software, they can effectively fix prices without ever talking to each other. The algorithm does the coordination. You can't opt out by switching stores.

Canada's Competition Bureau ran a formal public consultation on this in 2025. The majority of individual respondents called for regulation or outright banning. Manitoba's NDP government has already introduced a provincial bill. Now we need federal action.

Avi Lewis and the NDP are calling on the federal government to ban surveillance pricing — in-store and online. We think the price you pay should reflect the market, not your data profile.

Add your name to the petition → **ndp.ca/ban-surveillance-pricing**

Then share this post. The more people know what's happening, the harder it is to ignore.
