# Surveillance Pricing: What It Is and Why It Has to Stop

**Vancouver Centre NDP | ndp.ca/ban-surveillance-pricing**
*Data and sources reviewed and verified as of April 15, 2026.*

---

## What is surveillance pricing?

Surveillance pricing is when a company uses your personal data — where you live, what you browse, how often you shop, your purchase history — to figure out the maximum price you personally will pay, and then charges you that.

Not the market price. Your price.

Two people standing in the same store, or looking at the same product online, can be charged two different amounts — based on what the algorithm has learned about each of them.

---

## How does it work?

Retailers and tech companies collect data about you constantly: through your loyalty card, your phone's location, your browsing history, your past purchases, and data purchased from third-party brokers. An AI algorithm processes all of it to estimate your "willingness to pay" — and sets a price accordingly.

The Canada Competition Bureau ran a formal public consultation on this in 2025. What they heard: the practice is spreading into groceries, rental housing, ride-sharing, concert tickets, telecom, utilities, gas, and pharmaceuticals.

---

## Who gets hurt most?

The people the algorithm hits hardest are the people who can least afford it.

**If your child is sick at 2am** and you search for a thermometer or children's medicine, the algorithm sees urgency: late-night search, limited nearby options, stressed parent. Your panic is its pricing signal.

**If someone in your family dies**, the algorithm sees last-minute flights, inflexible travel, grief-driven decisions. Bereavement fares exist precisely because airlines have long charged more when you have no choice.

**If you're a new parent**, the moment you buy diapers twice from the same retailer, the algorithm knows you'll be back. Formula, baby medicine, wipes — prices creep up because the algorithm has learned you'll pay it.

**If you're a low-income shopper** with a predictable routine — same store, same time, same items — you're actually *easier* for the algorithm to profile. Consistency reads as captivity. The algorithm sees your necessity as an opportunity.

---

## Your loyalty card is a surveillance tool

When you scan your loyalty card, you earn points — worth cents. The store gets a detailed map of your shopping habits, your health needs, your routines, and your vulnerabilities. That data feeds the pricing algorithm.

The points you earn are worth far less than the data you're surrendering.

---

## The hidden collusion problem

When multiple grocery chains use the same third-party pricing algorithm, they can effectively fix prices without ever talking to each other. The algorithm does the coordination. No conspiracy, no meeting — just the same software pointing all of them toward the same higher price.

Canada's Competition Bureau has identified this as a serious emerging threat to fair competition.

---

## What the NDP is demanding

Manitoba's NDP government has already introduced a provincial bill to ban surveillance pricing. Now we need federal leadership to protect Canadians coast to coast — in-store and online.

New Democrats are calling on the federal government to ban the use of personal data to set individualized prices. The practice is "unfair and downright creepy" — and it has to stop before it becomes the norm.
