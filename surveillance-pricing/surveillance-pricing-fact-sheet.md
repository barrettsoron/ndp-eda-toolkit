# Surveillance Pricing: What It Is and Why It Has to Stop

**Vancouver Centre NDP | ndp.ca/ban-surveillance-pricing**
*Data and sources reviewed and verified as of April 24, 2026.*

---

## What is surveillance pricing?

Surveillance pricing is when a company uses your personal data — where you live, what you browse, how often you shop, your purchase history — to figure out the maximum price you personally will pay, and then charges you that.

Not the market price. Your price.

---

## How the price finds you — online and in-store

**Online.** Two people looking at the same product on the same site can be shown two different prices. The algorithm knows your account, your device, your location, your history — and sets your price before you click buy.

**In-store.** The shelf price isn't a neutral baseline — it's the ceiling. Your "personal price" is that ceiling, minus whatever relief the algorithm has decided you deserve on your phone. The shelf tag itself can now change many times a day.

Who pays the most isn't random. It's the shoppers with the weakest position in the algorithm's eyes:

- **Shoppers without the app** — no account, no phone, no data to trade — pay the full ceiling. Every time. Often older, often lower-income.
- **Predictable, loyal shoppers** get *worse* targeted offers, not better. The algorithm knows you're coming back either way. Retention discounts are spent on shoppers who might leave.
- **Shoppers flagged as necessity buyers** — diapers, medicine, formula, special-diet items — see prices walked up over time, because the data says you'll pay it.
- **Electronic shelf labels** let retailers raise the ceiling in real time, tuned to when the store is busy, when competitors are closed, or when the weather means nobody wants to drive farther. Loblaws, Sobeys, and Walmart Canada are rolling these out. Sobeys is completing its Canadian rollout this month.

A September 2025 *Consumer Reports* investigation put 400 volunteer shoppers into groups buying identical Instacart baskets. Seventy-five percent of products were offered at different prices to different customers. Identical baskets ranged from $114 to $124 for the same items — a gap that compounds to roughly $1,200 a year, on top of food bills already running $17,500 for a family of four in 2026. The AI pricing tool was called Eversight; Instacart acquired it in 2022 and stopped using it in December 2025, after the investigation ran.

Every loyalty card scan, app open, and store-Wi-Fi ping also trains the profile that prices you online. The "regular" price and the "personal" price aren't alternatives — they're two parts of one engineered system, both designed to find the maximum each shopper will pay.

---

## How does the data get collected?

Retailers and tech companies collect data about you constantly: through your loyalty card, your phone's location, your browsing history, your past purchases, and data purchased from third-party brokers. An AI algorithm processes all of it to estimate your "willingness to pay" — and sets a price accordingly.

The Canada Competition Bureau ran a formal public consultation in 2025. In January 2026, it published findings: more than 60 companies in Canada were already using algorithms to optimize retail pricing — including vendors already contracted to the country's largest grocery chains. The bureau found the practice spreading into groceries, rental housing, ride-sharing, concert tickets, telecom, utilities, gas, and pharmaceuticals. It declined to recommend regulatory action.

## Who gets hurt most?

The people the algorithm hits hardest are the people who can least afford it.

**If your child is sick at 2am** and you search for a thermometer or children's medicine, the algorithm sees urgency: late-night search, limited nearby options, stressed parent. Your panic is its pricing signal.

**If someone in your family dies**, the algorithm sees last-minute flights, inflexible travel, grief-driven decisions. Bereavement fares exist precisely because airlines have long charged more when you have no choice.

**If you're a new parent**, the moment you buy diapers twice from the same retailer, the algorithm knows you'll be back. Formula, baby medicine, wipes — prices creep up because the algorithm has learned you'll pay it.

**If you're a low-income shopper** with a predictable routine — same store, same time, same items — you're actually *easier* for the algorithm to profile. Consistency reads as captivity. The algorithm sees your necessity as an opportunity.

---

## Your loyalty card is a surveillance tool

When you scan your loyalty card, you earn points — worth cents. The store gets a detailed map of your shopping habits, your health needs, your routines, and your vulnerabilities. That data feeds the pricing algorithm.

The points you earn are worth far less than the data you're surrendering.

---

## The hidden collusion problem

When multiple grocery chains use the same third-party pricing algorithm, they can effectively fix prices without ever talking to each other. The algorithm does the coordination. No conspiracy, no meeting — just the same software pointing all of them toward the same higher price.

Canada's Competition Bureau has identified this as a serious emerging threat to fair competition.

## What the NDP is demanding

Manitoba's NDP government has already introduced a provincial bill to ban surveillance pricing — Bill 49, the first Canadian jurisdiction to require algorithmic pricing disclosure, in-store and online. Ontario NDP leader Marit Stiles tabled a non-binding motion calling for a federal ban — defeated at Queen's Park. Ontario Liberal interim leader John Fraser separately introduced a private member's bill to ban "personalized algorithmic pricing," awaiting second reading. UFCW Canada — representing 250,000 grocery, restaurant, and retail workers — threw its support behind the federal ban. A March 2026 Abacus Data poll found more than half of Canadians — 52% — consider algorithmic pricing unfair once it's explained to them.

Now we need federal leadership to protect Canadians coast to coast — in-store and online. New Democrats are calling on the federal government to ban the use of personal data to set individualized prices. The practice is "unfair and downright creepy" — and it has to stop before it becomes the norm.

---

## What Parliament has done — and hasn't

On April 13, 2026, MP Leah Gazan (Winnipeg Centre) tabled Motion M-30, calling on the federal government to ban surveillance pricing. On April 15, NDP Parliamentary Leader Don Davies asked the House to agree by unanimous consent — the Liberals blocked it. On April 16, M-30 came to a vote and the Liberal majority defeated it.

The Liberals' position: the Competition Bureau is studying the issue. The bureau has already found more than 60 companies in Canada using algorithmic pricing tools — and declined to recommend action. A study is not a ban, and it doesn't stop the practice while it proceeds.

A federal ban remains NDP policy. The petition builds the public record and the groundwork for an official parliamentary petition that an MP can table in the House.

Every signature demonstrates that Canadians are paying attention.
