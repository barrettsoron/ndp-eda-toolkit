## What's being built?

Beacon AI Centers (Calgary) and VoltaGrid (Texas) are proposing an AI data centre with a total power draw of roughly **390 megawatts** — 190 MW from an on-site VoltaGrid natural-gas plant, and an expected 200 MW from NB Power's grid — on a 120-hectare parcel at Lorneville, in the Spruce Lake Industrial Park, west of Saint John. The total announced investment is up to $2 billion. First operations are targeted for early 2028.

The on-site gas plant alone would emit roughly **755,000 tonnes of CO₂ per year** according to the project's filings — placing it among the top three single-source emitters in New Brunswick, alongside the Irving Oil refinery in Saint John and NB Power's Belledune generating station. The footprint includes the destruction of approximately **27 hectares of high-functioning wetlands** and significant tracts of forest that the proponent's own filings describe as old-growth.

The federal Impact Assessment Agency of Canada is reviewing the project under the Impact Assessment Act.

---

## Who is Beacon AI Centers?

Beacon AI Centers is a Calgary-based developer; VoltaGrid is a Texas-based modular gas-power and digital-infrastructure firm that supplies the on-site generation. Public details about Beacon's ownership, financing, and anchor tenants — the AI companies that would actually rent the compute — have not been disclosed at the time of this fact sheet.

The project has been publicly championed by **Frank McKenna**, former premier of New Brunswick, who has framed Saint John as a "hidden gem" for AI infrastructure investment. McKenna's endorsement carries weight provincially. It is not a substitute for an environmental assessment, a tenant disclosure, or a public conversation about whether 390 megawatts of combined gas and grid demand — and the infrastructure lock-in that comes with it — is the New Brunswick we want to build.

The *Tenant Explainer* later in this package will be filled in as the financing and tenant picture clarifies through the federal review.

## What are the concerns?

**A 390 MW load on a grid trying to decarbonise.** New Brunswick's electricity mix is shifting — Belledune is on the federal coal phase-out path, and NB Power has been planning a transition built around hydro, nuclear at Point Lepreau, and (regrettably, but at smaller scale than this) new gas. This project hits that grid from two directions at once: 190 MW of new private gas generation adds fossil emissions directly, and the 200 MW grid draw forces NB Power to find and hold capacity it would otherwise be retiring or redirecting. Once infrastructure of this scale is committed, it operates for decades. The emissions don't decline because the grid around it gets cleaner.

**Top-three emitter — for what?** At 755,000 tonnes of CO₂ per year, this single project would represent roughly 6% of New Brunswick's total annual emissions. The proponent projects roughly **1,300 construction jobs (temporary) and 210 permanent positions** once operational. A facility producing the emissions of a major oil refinery, with the permanent workforce of a medium-sized grocery store, is not a development trade most communities would freely make if they were asked.

**Wetlands and well water.** Twenty-seven hectares of high-functioning wetlands are slated for destruction. Wetlands are not just bird habitat — they regulate water tables, filter contaminants, and protect the private wells that residents in Lorneville and the surrounding rural communities rely on for drinking water. Wetland loss and groundwater impact in this area cannot be undone by a mitigation plan signed years later.

**A federal review with the proponent at the wheel.** The Impact Assessment Agency of Canada is the right body for a project of this scale, and the federal review is open. But the technical record the review depends on is largely written by the proponent. Independent expert review, full tenant disclosure, and meaningful Indigenous consultation are the difference between a federal process that legitimates a fait accompli and one that actually answers the questions Saint John residents are asking.

**Canadian infrastructure, American jurisdiction.** Canadian data centres serving American AI companies operate under American law. Under the US CLOUD Act, US authorities can compel a US-incorporated company to hand over data it controls regardless of where the data physically sits. When President Trump sanctioned the International Criminal Court's chief prosecutor in 2025, Microsoft notified the ICC it was required to comply with US sanctions obligations — Khan's account was suspended, forcing him to move to Proton Mail. When the French Senate asked Microsoft France under oath whether it could guarantee it would refuse a US government request for data held in European data centres, the company answered that it "cannot guarantee" that. The steel and concrete being proposed for Lorneville don't change the legal regime running the servers inside.

This concern depends on who Beacon's anchor tenants turn out to be. If the tenants are US-incorporated, the sovereignty question is real. If they are Canadian, the question shifts. Beacon has not publicly answered it.

## What about water?

Beacon and VoltaGrid have publicly stated the facility "would not guzzle city water" and have pointed to closed-loop cooling and dry-cooling configurations to address concerns about municipal supply. The framing is correct as far as it goes — air-cooled and closed-loop systems do not consume water the way evaporative-cooled facilities do.

The trade-offs are what proponents tend not to volunteer. Closed-loop and dry-cooling systems require **more electricity** to move the same heat — which on a project served by a dedicated 190 MW gas plant means more gas burned and more emissions per unit of compute. And "would not guzzle city water" is a separate question from impacts on local groundwater, surface water in Spruce Lake, and the private wells around Lorneville that the wetland destruction puts at risk.

For context, globally, data centre water consumption is projected to rise from roughly 560 billion litres per year to 1,200 billion litres by 2030 according to the International Energy Agency. The "no water" framing on individual projects has to be read against that backdrop — and against the fact that the binding constraint here is not water, it is carbon and wetland.

## What is the NDP calling for?

NDP Leader Avi Lewis has committed to a [**Humans-First AI Policy**](https://lewisisleader.ca/ideas/dignified-work) that includes **pausing the expansion of AI data centres in Canada** — to protect clean water and wetland systems, to preserve energy for human health and wellbeing, and to give our democratic institutions time to catch up with a technology that has been deployed without public debate. This is a federal ask because the load-bearing questions — national security, data sovereignty, cross-border data flows, the regulation of grid-scale fossil generation, and who owns Canadian AI infrastructure — are federal in nature. The Lorneville project is the proximate trigger. The concern is national.

Specific federal tools include amending the Canadian Environmental Protection Act to regulate data centre environmental impacts, conditioning federal funding on tenant disclosure and meaningful consultation, and levying a tax on data centres that internalises grid and emissions costs.

The NDP draws a clear line between small-scale, publicly useful machine learning — medical research, public-service applications, accessibility — and the multi-billion-dollar corporate AI industry built on generative AI, mass data extraction, and the displacement of millions of workers.

---

## Who benefits? Who pays?

The $2 billion investment benefits Beacon AI Centers, VoltaGrid, the still-unnamed AI companies that will rent the compute, and the local boosters who have already committed to it as a vision for Saint John's economic future. The 755,000 tonnes of annual CO₂, the 27 hectares of wetland loss, the old-growth forest loss, the pressure on private wells, the noise, and the reputational cost of hosting one of the country's largest single-source emitters stay with Lorneville, the surrounding communities, and New Brunswick as a whole.

Two hundred and ten permanent jobs is a real number. It is also a number that has to be set honestly against the scale of what it is being asked to justify.

The petition this package supports asks the federal government to pause the expansion of AI data centres in Canada — starting with the Lorneville project — until that conversation has happened on terms the community sets, not on a timeline the proponent does.
