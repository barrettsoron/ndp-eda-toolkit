# Canada Strong Fund — NDP Riding Association Organizing Kit

**Audience:** Vancouver Centre EDA exec, local membership, and other federal NDP riding executives across BC and the country. Secondary audience: members and supporters who already agree on the values but need the language to make the case in their own kitchens, doorsteps, and locals.

**Goal:** Push the federal NDP caucus, Leader's Office, and policy apparatus to adopt a sharp, ideologically clear position on the Canada Strong Fund — both/and: replace the fund's funding mechanism and design with the platform's already-proposed sovereign wealth fund, and transform what passes from inside through binding conditions on climate, Indigenous rights, union labour, and public ownership.

**Posture:** This is not reflexive opposition. The Liberals are using language that belongs to the broader progressive tradition — public ownership, nation-building, Indigenous partnership, sovereign wealth fund — to package policy that inverts those traditions' design choices. Our job is to take that language back, with policy that already exists in Avi's platform.

---

## Avi's framing

From Avi's statement on the Spring Economic Statement (2026-04-28):

> *"This is exactly what the Prime Minister's Sovereign Wealth Fund does. It's a mirror image of Norway's successful model: Norway invests revenue from its public oil company into its fund for the public's benefit. Prime Minister Carney's approach is to seed the fund with public money to subsidize big private projects."*
>
> *"In other words, Norway uses industry profits to support the public good. Prime Minister Carney's fund uses public money to support private profits."*
>
> *"I have an idea for the Prime Minister: why don't we pull a Norway? Let's put a windfall tax on oil and gas companies that are projected to make $90B in profits this year because of Trump's illegal war in Iran. Then, let's use that money to fill up the Sovereign Wealth fund — and invest in projects that benefit Canadians instead of a few wealthy investors and CEOs."*

That is the entire fight in three sentences. **Public money in, private profits out** (Carney). **Industry profits in, public good out** (Norway, and Avi's platform). Same instrument, opposite purposes.

## Part 1 — The strategic frame

### What Carney announced (in plain language)

On April 27, in front of steam locomotives at the Canada Science and Technology Museum, the PM announced a $25-billion Crown corporation that takes **minority equity stakes** in private projects — ports, pipelines, mines, LNG, nuclear — alongside Bay Street pension funds and global asset managers like Brookfield and BlackRock. The state takes the first-loss risk; private capital takes the operating control and most of the upside. The retail product (the "people's fund" feature) lets Canadians who already have surplus savings buy what amounts to a government bond with a small kicker. The fund's written mandate contains zero references to climate, Indigenous rights, UNDRIP, FPIC, just transition, or union labour.

The Spring Economic Statement tabled the next day fails the moment more broadly: virtually no new measures on cost of living, no new revenue by taxing excessive corporate profits, and a pattern of throwing public money at private capital in housing, infrastructure, and beyond. **The CSF is the most visible instance of that pattern, not an isolated bad design.**

Carney calls it Norway. It isn't. Norway's fund is seeded by revenue from a publicly-owned oil company and redistributes those rents through a 3% spending rule into a redistributive welfare state, with hard-coded ethical exclusions on coal, weapons, and human rights violators. Carney's fund is seeded by public money — taxpayer dollars — and routes that capital to private project sponsors who keep the operating profits. It is, as Avi puts it, a **mirror image** of the Norwegian model.

### Why this fight matters now

The legislative window is short. The Transition Office is being stood up. Board appointments are weeks away. Enabling legislation will be drafted before summer. The first project announcements will come this fall. Every progressive concession has to be written into statute before the cement sets — once the fund is operating on a "fully commercial" mandate with a Bay Street board, the fight to add climate or Indigenous criteria afterward becomes nearly impossible. **This is the most consequential federal economic-policy fight since the 2017 Canada Infrastructure Bank legislation, and the price tag is bigger.**

### The strategic opening

The Liberals are running on language that belongs to us. "Sovereign wealth fund." "Nation-building." "Public ownership." "Indigenous partnership." Carney is using social-democratic rhetoric to package a Wall Street Consensus instrument. **This creates an opening, not a defeat** — the values terrain is ours, the policy is in Avi's platform, and the architecture doesn't match the rhetoric. Our job is to make the gap visible.

The both/and posture: **publicly demand the maximum** (a Norway-style sovereign wealth fund seeded by a windfall tax on oil and gas profits, alongside the network of Crown corporations Avi's platform already specifies); **legislatively push the minimum** (binding climate exclusions, FPIC requirements, union content, Canadian content, public majority stakes, democratic governance) into whatever passes.

## Part 2 — The message stack

Six core messages, each with a one-line frame, a paragraph of substance, and a sample doorstep version. These are designed to be remixable — pick the two or three that fit the audience.

### Message 1: Pull a Norway. Public money in, private profits out — that's not how a sovereign wealth fund works.

**Frame:** Norway's fund — the one Carney keeps invoking — is seeded by *industry profits* from a *publicly-owned oil company* and pays those rents back to the *public good*. Carney's fund inverts every part of that. It uses public money to subsidize private projects, with returns that flow to Bay Street pension funds and asset managers.

**Substance:** This is Avi's central charge, and it's the cleanest line in the whole fight. *Norway uses industry profits to support the public good. Carney's fund uses public money to support private profits.* The platform already proposed how to do it the right way — a windfall profits tax on the oil and gas companies projected to make $90B this year on the back of Trump's illegal war in Iran, with that revenue feeding a sovereign wealth fund mandated to share resource wealth with impacted and Indigenous communities for generations. **The instrument is fine. The funding source and the design are everything.**

**Doorstep version:** "Carney calls it the Norway model. It's the opposite of the Norway model. Norway taxes the oil companies and puts the money to public good. Carney is taking your money and putting it behind private projects. Avi's been clear: pull a Norway. Tax the oil profits. Use that revenue, not yours."

### Message 2: The label belongs to a long progressive tradition. The design choices are what matter — and Carney got them backward.

**Frame:** Sovereign wealth funds aren't new, and Avi didn't invent them. Norway, Alaska, and dozens of other jurisdictions have been running them for decades. What matters is the design — who pays in, what the fund's purpose is, who governs it, where the returns flow. Avi's platform makes specific choices on each of those questions. Carney makes the opposite choices, then borrows the legitimacy of the better-known examples to sell a fundamentally different instrument.

**Substance:** Avi's platform proposed a sovereign wealth fund as a narrow, specific tool — to share resource extraction wealth with impacted and Indigenous communities for generations, funded by the industry generating the rents. That's the Norway and Alaska tradition: industry pays in, public benefits, with hard-coded ethical guardrails. Carney's CSF inverts those design choices — taxpayer money pays in, private project sponsors benefit, no ethical guardrails in the statute. **The CSF also substitutes for the work the platform assigned to other tools** — the public corporations Avi proposed to build public housing, public power, and public pharmaceuticals directly. *"a new generation of public corporations – federal, provincial, municipal, and Indigenous"* doing direct production isn't an SWF's job. Carney is using one instrument to do work that requires several.

**Doorstep version:** "Sovereign wealth funds have been around for decades. The question is always the same: who pays in, who benefits. Norway's oil companies pay in; the public benefits. Carney's design has it backward — public money goes in, private projects benefit. And it's being used as a substitute for actually building public housing, public power, public pharmaceuticals. Wrong tool, wrong design."

### Message 3: This is not a people's fund. It's a Bay Street fund.

**Frame:** Carney is using "people's fund" rhetoric to describe a vehicle whose primary beneficiaries are pension fund managers, Brookfield-style asset managers, and the construction giants that build the projects. The "retail product" is a savings vehicle for Canadians who already have surplus capital — it's structurally regressive.

**Substance:** On the morning of the announcement, Finance Minister Champagne held a parallel event in Montreal where he briefed the leaders of Canada's largest banks, pension plans, and construction companies. No labour leaders. No climate organizations. No Indigenous rights-holders. No anti-poverty advocates. No municipal leaders. The Globe and Mail had to redact attendee names because they weren't authorized to speak. **That dinner is who this fund is built for.** A real people's fund would distribute returns universally as cash — the way Alaska's Permanent Fund Dividend has done since 1976, paying every resident annually regardless of whether they had savings to invest. Carney chose to copy Norway's name, not its design.

**Doorstep version:** "Carney calls it a people's fund. The morning of the announcement, his finance minister had breakfast with Bay Street, the big banks, and the construction giants. Labour wasn't there. Indigenous nations weren't there. Climate groups weren't there. The fund is built for the people who were in that room."

### Message 4: We've seen this movie. It's the Canada Infrastructure Bank with a bigger budget.

**Frame:** Canadians paid for the CIB experiment. We know what arm's-length federal investment vehicles deliver when their boards are recruited from RBC, Brookfield, Vancity, and the P3 ecosystem: chronic under-deployment, opaque accountability, and "private investment leverage" that the Parliamentary Budget Officer has repeatedly failed to find evidence of.

**Substance:** The CIB was supposed to deliver $35-billion in private-sector-led infrastructure leverage. After eight years, the PBO has found the leverage claims unverifiable. The board is dominated by the same closed network the CSF will draw from — Michael Sabia (CDPQ → CIB Chair → Deputy Minister of Finance → Hydro-Québec → Clerk of the Privy Council), Tamara Vrooman (Vancity → CIB Chair, self-described "big fan of public-private partnerships"), Macky Tall (CDPQ-Infra → Carlyle → CIB board). The CSF is the CIB upgraded to the asset-manager-capitalism era, with the same governance circuit and a bigger price tag. **An NDP that tolerates a second pass at this experiment is not learning from the first one.**

**Doorstep version:** "The Liberals already tried this with the Canada Infrastructure Bank — same arm's-length structure, same Bay Street board. After eight years, the PBO can't even prove it's mobilizing the private investment it promised. Now they want a $25-billion sequel."

### Message 5: This is fossil-bailout-with-green-paint at a moment of structural decline.

**Frame:** The fund's project pipeline is the Major Projects Office pipeline — which means LNG export terminals, a new bitumen pipeline, the Pathways Plus carbon-capture-and-store hub, and SMR nuclear — at the same moment Carney has killed the consumer carbon tax, cancelled the oil-and-gas emissions cap, and weakened greenwashing rules under Bill C-59.

**Substance:** The PMO release explicitly pairs "clean and conventional energy" as co-equal asset classes. The "transformative strategies" already moving through MPO include Pathways Plus (which mathematically *increases* total emissions when paired with a new pipeline of additional production) and the Ksi Lisims LNG project (whose lead equity owner is Blackstone/Apollo). The CSF's job is to put public capital into projects whose business model is structurally exposed by global LNG oversupply and declining Asian demand — converting public balance sheet into stranded-asset risk to bail out fossil capital that can no longer raise patient money on its own terms. **A real Green New Deal does not co-invest in the projects it is supposed to phase out.** Avi's platform is direct on this: *Powerlines Not Pipelines.* No federal approvals for new pipelines, offshore oil projects, or LNG terminals.

**Doorstep version:** "Carney killed the carbon tax, cancelled the emissions cap, and now wants $25-billion to put your money behind LNG terminals and a new bitumen pipeline. That's not climate policy. That's a bailout for the industry causing the problem."

### Message 6: Equity stakes are not Indigenous sovereignty.

**Frame:** Offering a minority equity stake to a First Nation in a project that violates the consent of other rights-holding Nations is not partnership — it's the manufacture of consent. The fund is built downstream of a Bill C-5 architecture that AFN, UBCIC, and the First Nations Leadership Council have called a betrayal of UNDRIP.

**Substance:** Bill C-5 passed in under a month with a seven-day "consultation" window. UBCIC Resolution 2025-24 declared it "in complete opposition to UNDRIP and Canada's own UNDA." AFN National Chief Cindy Woodhouse Nepinak said the process was "ramming something through." Mohawk Council of Kahnawà:ke Grand Chief Cody Diabo walked out of the post-hoc summit. There are real Indigenous-led, majority-Indigenous-owned projects worth supporting — Cedar LNG (Haisla 50.1%), Wataynikaneyap Power (24 First Nations), Tu Deh-Kah geothermal (Fort Nelson First Nation 100%). **The CSF as designed will be invited to interpret minority equity offers from individual Nations as proof of FPIC for whole projects.** That is the opposite of self-determination. A Crown corporation operating on stolen land and unceded territory has to clear the FPIC bar before it deploys capital, not after. Avi's platform is explicit: the SWF exists *to share the wealth generated from mining with impacted and Indigenous communities, to support them for generations to come* — distribution to rights-holders, not minority shares offered to manufacture consent.

**Doorstep version:** "Selling 12% of a pipeline to one First Nation while running over the consent of every other Nation in the territory isn't partnership. It's a strategy to manufacture the appearance of consent. UNDRIP requires the real thing."

## Part 3 — The five demands

These are the concrete legislative and policy changes to organize around. Designed for inclusion in MP letters, letters to the editor, social copy, and small-group conversations during the legislative window.

### Demand 1: Fund the SWF the way Avi's platform funds it — windfall taxes on oil and gas profits, not public seed money

The platform's commitment is unambiguous: *"institute a windfall profits tax to help finance the energy transition and sustainable jobs."* With Canadian oil and gas projected to clear $90-billion in profits this year on the back of Trump's Iran war, the revenue source exists. **The CSF should be capitalized by a windfall profits tax on oil and gas — not by $25-billion of taxpayer cash transferred to a Bay Street-managed Crown corp.** Pull a Norway: industry profits in, public good out.

### Demand 2: Bind the SWF's mandate to its platform purpose — sharing resource wealth with impacted and Indigenous communities, generationally

Avi's platform names the SWF's purpose precisely: *"a sovereign wealth fund to share the wealth generated from mining with impacted and Indigenous communities, to support them for generations to come."* That purpose has to be in the enabling statute. Returns flow to impacted communities (with co-governance by Indigenous Nations whose territories generate the rents), not to a Bay Street-managed retail product. Hard-coded ethical exclusions on the Norway Council on Ethics model: no fossil expansion, no projects without documented FPIC from all rights-holding Indigenous Nations whose territories are affected.

### Demand 3: Public majority stakes, not minority equity

Where the CSF invests directly in projects of genuine public benefit, it must take majority ownership and operating control — not the CDPQ-Infra/REM minority co-investment template. Public money buys public assets. The platform's vocabulary applies: *"federal, provincial, municipal, and Indigenous"* public corporations, not minority slivers of private deals.

### Demand 4: Union content, Canadian content, and democratic governance

Every funded project must meet binding union density thresholds (Master Labour Agreements with Canadian Building Trades Unions, no exemptions for "energy security"), Canadian-content thresholds for materials and equipment, and a project-labour-agreement requirement. The CSF board must include worker, Indigenous, and climate-civil-society representation by statute — not only Bay Street alumni.

### Demand 5: A public-ownership pillar alongside the fund — the Crown corporations Avi's platform actually specifies

The SWF cannot be the only economic vehicle. It must be paired with the public corporations Avi already proposed: a federal public builder for one million social, co-op, and non-profit homes in five years; *Connaught 2.0* for pharmaceuticals; postal banking through Canada Post; a network of public telecom providers on the SaskTel model; and *a new generation of green public corporations — federal, provincial, municipal, and Indigenous* — mass-producing heat pumps, solar, batteries, and electric buses. Plus a federal Just Transition Transfer for fossil-dependent communities and workers, on the platform's 1%-of-GDP Green Jobs Transfer commitment. **Avi's platform is the floor, not the ceiling.**

## Part 5 — Tactical notes for the EDA exec

A few things worth flagging to your fellow exec members and to the riding membership when this goes on an agenda.

**On internal NDP politics.** The federal caucus and the Leader's Office will face pressure to "support nation-building" and avoid appearing reflexively oppositional. Avi's framing solves this — we're not against the SWF, we're against *how Carney's funding it and what he's using it for.* "Pull a Norway" is the exact rhetorical move that lets the caucus be sharp without being wedged. The clearer we are on the architectural critique, the less the party can be pulled into either reflexive opposition or capitulation.

**On the language.** *"Pull a Norway"* is the headline frame and should appear in every doorstep conversation, social post, and short-form member touch. *"Public money in, private profits out"* / *"industry profits in, public good out"* is the analytical version that goes in LTE and MP-letter copy. *"Bay Street fund, not a people's fund"* is the supplementary frame for the elite-capture critique. Rotate based on audience.

**On the Bay Street/labour/Indigenous frame.** Don't let the messaging drift into pure technocratic critique. The most powerful version pairs the architectural critique (commercial-return mandate, no climate criteria, etc.) with the *who's at the table* critique (banks vs. labour, project sponsors vs. rights-holders, asset managers vs. communities). Class and consent, not just policy design.

**On the broader economic statement.** Avi's statement frames the CSF as the *most visible instance* of a pattern in the Spring Economic Statement, not a one-off. The full critique is: no new revenue measures, no new affordability measures, public money substituted for taxing the corporate hoarding class. The SWF fight is the wedge into that broader critique. Members making this argument should be ready to extend it to housing, infrastructure, and corporate subsidies generally.

**On the $90B number.** Avi cited $90-billion in projected oil and gas profits this year, attributable in part to Trump's Iran war driving up energy prices. That number is the heart of the windfall-tax case — it's the answer to "how would you pay for it." Get it into the talking points and have it ready for the inevitable "where would the money come from" question.

**On the timing.** The Spring Economic Update tabled on April 28 contains the legislative architecture. Whatever's in that document is the immediate target. The Transition Office staffing will be the next leading indicator. First board appointments tell you who the fund is really for.

**On the asks of caucus.** The clearest single ask is for NDP MPs to table a private member's amendment package containing the five demands above, to be voted on as a clear yes/no on each. This is the legislative version of the message stack — it forces every other MP to vote on each demand individually, which makes the politics legible.

**Final note.** Avi's platform was the most ideologically coherent campaign document the federal NDP has produced in a generation. The Carney announcement is the perfect terrain to demonstrate why — because the Liberals are running on counterfeit versions of our policies, and the gap between the rhetoric and the architecture is exactly where we organize. *Pull a Norway* is the doorstep version of that whole argument: short, memorable, positive demand, grounded in a real working model, and pointing directly at platform commitments members already endorsed. The choir already knows the song. This is the sheet music.
