## About this document

The Regina module's `tenant-explainer.md` covers the *anchor tenants* (CoreWeave, Cerebras) renting capacity from Bell. The Saint John module documents the proponent (Beacon AI Centers) and notes anchor tenants as undisclosed. For Vancouver, the picture is structurally similar to Saint John: a Canadian-flag proponent (TELUS, with Westbank as developer), a federal program seal of approval, and **no publicly named anchor tenants** as of the May 13, 2026 announcement.

This document covers what is known and explicitly flags what is not.

---

## TELUS — the proponent

TELUS Corporation is one of Canada's three large telecommunications companies, headquartered in Vancouver and led by CEO **Darren Entwistle** since 2000. TELUS already operates a data centre in Kamloops; the May 13 announcement is the company's largest single infrastructure expansion in BC in recent years.

TELUS's stated rationale for the cluster: capturing demand from "Canadian businesses, entrepreneurs, startups, researchers and, yes, government at all levels" (Entwistle, May 13, 2026). The company has not disclosed the project cost, citing competitive reasons.

TELUS is publicly traded (TSX: T, NYSE: TU). Its largest shareholders are institutional — Canadian and US asset managers, Canadian pension funds. TELUS is incorporated in BC and is a Canadian company under federal law.

The Canadian-incorporation point is load-bearing. If TELUS itself were the AI compute customer, the *Sovereign AI* framing of the federal program would be on solid ground. It is not. TELUS is the **landlord**. The customers — the firms that will rent the GPUs and run the workloads — are not disclosed.

---

## Westbank Corp. — the developer

Westbank is a privately held Vancouver real estate developer founded by Ian Gillespie in 1992. Its portfolio includes Vancouver House, Telus Garden, the Oakridge Park redevelopment, and Mirvish Village in Toronto. Westbank is the developer partner on both proposed Vancouver sites: the Mount Pleasant office conversion of the M3 building (111 East 5th Ave) and the proposed new downtown facility at 150 West Georgia / 720 Beatty St beside BC Place. As of May 18, 2026, no formal rezoning application for the downtown data centre use has been reported as submitted to the City.

The downtown facility is integrated with the **Creative Energy** district-energy system, which operates the steam-loop network that heats more than 200 buildings on the downtown peninsula. The data centre is planned to sit atop an expanded and replaced steam plant being built below-grade on the same parcel; the heat-rejection design appears to envisage some integration with the district-energy system, though specifics have not been published.

**Allied Properties REIT** is publicly reported to be involved in the downtown site as a lender and equity partner. Westbank itself is privately held; its capital structure is not public.

## The federal program — *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres*

The program sits inside Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada (ISED). It assessed 160 proposals on six criteria: **sovereignty, economic benefit, performance, energy, readiness, and cost**. TELUS is the first publicly approved project. Federal contribution amounts have not been disclosed. ISED has indicated a Memorandum of Understanding will follow current negotiations.

The *Sovereign* framing in the program name is the contested ground. ISED's working definition appears to centre on the proponent's incorporation (Canadian) and the data centre's physical location (in Canada). Civil society and academic critics (and the NDP) have argued sovereignty in the AI context requires more: anchor-tenant Canadian incorporation, Canadian law as the operational legal regime, and meaningful Canadian regulatory oversight of the workloads run.

AI Minister **Evan Solomon** announced the program approval alongside TELUS on May 13. The minister has framed the program as "the kind of ambitious infrastructure we need as a country" and has not, in public statements to date, addressed the absence of disclosed tenants.

## Anchor tenants — pending

**Not disclosed as of May 18, 2026.** TELUS's announcement materials describe a "Canadian business ecosystem" of intended customers — researchers, startups, government — without naming any. The proposed 60,000-GPU, 150-MW capacity is far in excess of what disclosed Canadian customers in any of those categories require. Hyperscale capacity at this scale, in current global market conditions, is built for a small number of large customers. Industry pattern in Canada to date (Bell / CoreWeave / Cerebras at Regina; Beacon / undisclosed at Saint John) suggests US-incorporated AI firms as the most likely fit.

This file should be updated when tenant disclosure occurs — either voluntarily by TELUS, through ISED's Memorandum of Understanding negotiations, or via a parliamentary committee or media disclosure.

## Financing

- **TELUS:** project cost not disclosed; financed on TELUS's balance sheet plus federal contribution (amount not disclosed).
- **Westbank:** capital structure for the two Vancouver sites not public.
- **Federal:** contribution amount under the *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres* program not disclosed; MoU pending.
- **BC Hydro:** initial 85 MW of generation secured; commercial terms not public.

The opacity is the issue. Federal money — public money — is being committed to a project whose tenant identity, cost, and federal contribution amount are all still confidential.

## US / foreign control concerns

The CLOUD Act argument from the Regina module applies in the same shape here, conditional on tenant identity. If TELUS leases substantial GPU capacity to US-incorporated AI firms (OpenAI, Anthropic, Cohere-via-US-parent, CoreWeave, Cerebras, AWS, Microsoft, Google, Meta), then under the US CLOUD Act those firms are compellable by US authorities to produce data they control regardless of where the underlying servers sit.

Two precedents to keep in mind:

- When US President Trump sanctioned the International Criminal Court's chief prosecutor Karim Khan in 2025, **Microsoft** notified the ICC it was required to comply with US sanctions and Khan's account was suspended. He moved to Proton Mail.
- When the **French Senate** asked Microsoft France under oath whether it could guarantee it would refuse a US government request for data held in European data centres, the company answered that it "cannot guarantee" that.

Canadian-flag steel and concrete in Vancouver does not change the legal regime running the workloads. The question that determines whether *Sovereign AI* is meaningful or marketing is **who the tenants are**.
