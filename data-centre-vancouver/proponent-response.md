## About this document

The Regina module includes a claim-by-claim rebuttal of Bell's `businessmarkets.bell.ca/regina-info` page. TELUS has not yet published an analogous standalone project page; the framings below are drawn from the May 13, 2026 announcement materials, TELUS's media interviews and statements through that week, and TELUS's May 25, 2026 follow-up statement to *Daily Hive* responding to the May 23 protest — its first sustained operational defence of the cluster. **Update this file when TELUS publishes a permanent project information page.**

## Claim 1: "Sovereign Canadian AI infrastructure"

**TELUS framing.** The cluster is part of the federal *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres* program. Minister Evan Solomon: infrastructure designed for "Canadian companies building things that will function under Canadian law, free from the coercion of others."

**Response.** Sovereignty in the AI infrastructure context depends on **who runs the workloads**, not who owns the building. TELUS has not disclosed a single anchor tenant. The proposed 60,000-GPU, 150-MW capacity exceeds disclosed Canadian demand. Industry pattern in Canada in 2026 — Bell at Regina hosting CoreWeave and Cerebras; Beacon at Saint John still undisclosed — suggests US-incorporated AI firms as the most likely actual users. Until tenants are disclosed and Canadian-incorporated, *Sovereign* is a marketing claim, not a technical or legal one.

If TELUS leases substantial capacity to US firms, those firms are compellable under the **US CLOUD Act** regardless of where the steel sits. See `tenant-explainer.md` for the Microsoft/ICC and Microsoft France/Senate precedents.

## Claim 2: "98 per cent renewable BC Hydro energy"

**TELUS framing.** All three projects will run on 98% renewable BC Hydro electricity (BetaKit, May 13).

**Response.** True as a description of BC Hydro's typical generation mix on a typical year. **Not true** as a description of the marginal source serving a new 150 MW load on a grid still importing power. BC Hydro's 2025/26 second-quarter report shows net electricity imports of 2,041 GWh in the six months ending September 30, 2025 — down 56% from the drought-driven prior year, but still material. Imports flow from Alberta and the US Pacific Northwest via the integrated Western Interconnection, where marginal generation remains coal- and gas-heavy. New hyperscale load in these conditions is served by the marginal source — not by the average renewable mix.

Adding 150 MW of compute load also competes directly with the electrification BC needs for housing (heat pumps), transit, and industry. Every megawatt allocated to GPU racks is a megawatt unavailable for those uses without further imports or new generation.

## Claim 3: "90 per cent lower water consumption" (downtown site)

**TELUS framing.** The 150 West Georgia facility will use a closed-loop liquid cooling system, with water consumption "targeted to be 90 per cent lower than traditional data centres."

**Response.** Closed-loop cooling does substantially reduce water consumption versus evaporative cooling — the technology is real. Three caveats:

1. **Targeted ≠ verified.** The 90% figure is a design target, not a measured outcome. Independent verification post-commissioning matters.
2. **One site of three.** The Mount Pleasant office conversion and the Kamloops expansion have not been described in the same cooling-architecture detail. Cluster-wide water consumption cannot be calculated from one site's headline number.
3. **Closed-loop trades water for electricity.** Moving the same heat without evaporation requires more chiller and air-side rejection capacity, which means more electricity. In a grid deficit, that means more imported gas/coal — substituting one environmental cost for another.

Metro Vancouver entered 2026 in continuing drought, with Stage 2 water restrictions in force as of late May 2026. The water question is real. The 90% figure addresses part of it for one building.

**Update — May 25, 2026.** In a follow-up statement to *Daily Hive*, TELUS quantified the saving as **"300 million litres saved each year."** Two things to note. **Against what baseline:** EarthJustice cites traditional data centres at 1–5 million gallons per day, or roughly 1,400–7,000 million litres per year *per facility*. TELUS's 300 ML/yr saved implies a traditional baseline of around 333 ML/yr — well below the EarthJustice figure for a single hyperscale centre — suggesting the comparison is to a much smaller reference facility, or to one site only rather than the cluster. **And at what scale:** Metro Vancouver consumes roughly 1 billion litres of water per day; 300 ML/yr saved is less than a single day of regional consumption. Real, but not large at regional scale, and the comparison should be made explicit before the figure is cited.

## Claim 4: "$9 billion in economic activity, 1,000 construction jobs, 525 permanent positions"

**TELUS framing.** $9 billion in claimed economic activity for BC; 1,000+ construction jobs; "hundreds of long-term technology and operations positions" (525 specified in BetaKit reporting).

**Response.** Economic activity figures of this kind are typically gross (counting every dollar that flows through the project, with no netting against displacement of other investment) and use multipliers chosen for a marketing context. Treat the $9 billion as the **largest defensible upper bound** TELUS could publish, not as a forecast.

525 permanent positions across three sites — one in Kamloops, two in Vancouver — is a real number but should be set honestly against the scale of grid and water commitment being asked. Saint John (Beacon, 390 MW) projects 210 permanent positions at a similar level of grid disruption. Regina (Bell, 300 MW) is in the same range. Hyperscale data centres are not employment-dense facilities. The case for them is a national-infrastructure case, not a jobs case. It should be argued that way and judged that way.

## Claim 5: "Municipal approval secured" (downtown site)

**TELUS / BetaKit framing.** Municipal approval secured for the 150 West Georgia data centre.

**Response.** This conflates two different projects. In **October 2020**, Vancouver City Council unanimously approved a Westbank rezoning at 720 Beatty St (now addressed 150 W Georgia) for a **17-storey, 583,243 sq ft office tower** designed by **Bjarke Ingels Group with HCMA Architecture & Design**, sited atop the expanded Creative Energy district-energy steam plant. That project stalled when the office market weakened. The current proposal — a **10-storey purpose-built data centre as the anchor use within a broader ~700,000 sq ft mixed-use concept** — is a substantively different building program on the same parcel. Public reporting as of May 18, 2026 describes the data centre as a recent pivot announced through Westbank's fiscal disclosures, with **BC Hydro committing 39 MW with a path to 50 MW and a potential 100 MW for the downtown site**. No formal rezoning application for the data centre use has been reported as submitted to the City.

In other words: the data centre as a use has **not** been approved by Council. A 2020 office-tower rezoning has. Treating one as the other elides the central question — does Vancouver want a hyperscale data centre on this parcel? — by smuggling it under an approval the public consented to for something else.

**Vancouver City Councillor Sean Orr** has publicly questioned whether data centres represent the best use of city infrastructure. A formal rezoning would put that question to public hearing. The federal *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres* program, meanwhile, has not been to a parliamentary committee.

## Claim 6: "First approved applicant" / "ambitious infrastructure"

**TELUS / federal framing.** Solomon: this is the first project "we are announcing publicly that we are moving ahead and advancing." Reflects "the kind of ambitious infrastructure we need as a country."

**Response.** Ambition is fine. Speed-of-approval relative to public debate is the issue. ISED's program assessed 160 proposals on six criteria; the assessment matrix is not public; the tenants are not disclosed; the federal contribution is not disclosed; parliamentary review has not happened; First Nations consultation on the Vancouver sites has not been documented. The first project under a major new federal program is the moment to set the standard. Approving it before the public has seen the program's terms inverts that.

## Claim 7: "80 per cent more energy-efficient than a traditional data centre"

**TELUS framing.** Per TELUS's May 25, 2026 statement to *Daily Hive*, the Vancouver facilities will be "80 per cent more energy-efficient than a traditional data centre."

**Response.** This describes a Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) improvement — the share of facility power going to cooling and overhead rather than compute. Traditional PUE runs ~1.6–1.8; modern liquid-cooled facilities ~1.1–1.2. A drop from 1.7 to 1.1 cuts *overhead* by roughly 85% but reduces *total facility power per unit of compute* by ~35%. The efficiency gain is real — and it does not reduce the compute load itself.

The 26 MW (Mount Pleasant) and 100 MW (downtown) figures TELUS has announced **already include** this efficiency gain. The grid impact is what it is; the 80% figure does not shrink it. And per-square-foot facility power is going *up*, not down: AI compute racks pull 50–130 kW each, many times what conventional server racks draw. The efficiency is per unit of compute. The absolute load is the largest the building has ever carried.

## Claim 8: "Heating the equivalent of 150,000 homes" via waste heat capture

**TELUS framing.** Waste heat will be captured and fed into Vancouver's Neighbourhood Energy Utility in Mount Pleasant and Creative Energy's downtown district energy system, "heating the equivalent of 150,000 homes in Metro Vancouver" — "every electron twice."

**Response.** Heat capture into district energy is a genuine virtue when it works. Three things the headline does not address:

1. **Seasonal mismatch.** Heat is useful in winter. Summer waste heat has no space-heating demand and must be rejected to atmosphere or removed by additional chiller load. "150,000 homes" is an annualized gross thermal-output figure, not a delivered heating service. Real homes don't run on July heat.
2. **Distribution infrastructure does not exist at that scale.** Vancouver's Neighbourhood Energy Utility serves Olympic Village; Creative Energy's downtown system serves the downtown core. Neither currently reaches anything close to 150,000 dwellings. The pipe network to deliver heat to that many homes is not part of this project, not budgeted, and not announced.
3. **Low-grade heat requires upgrading.** Liquid cooling rejects heat at roughly 30–40°C; district heating supply runs ~70–90°C. Bridging that gap takes heat pumps consuming additional electricity. Free heat in the brochure; not free heat in the system.

Heat capture as a design feature is welcome. **"Heating 150,000 homes" as a delivered outcome would require infrastructure that is not part of this project, on a timeline that has not been disclosed.** Until the receiving district-energy utilities publish a scaled commitment to take, upgrade, and deliver that heat, the figure is a theoretical gross thermal maximum — not a service.

One structural note worth carrying alongside the figure: **Creative Energy** — the downtown district-energy system named as the heat-capture recipient — was acquired by Westbank in 2014 (when Westbank bought Central Heat Distribution for $32M and rebranded it). So the data centre's developer, the parcel under the steam plant, and the utility that would buy and resell the captured heat are all parts of the same private group; only the compute operator (TELUS) is separately owned. The promise of "every electron twice" depends on infrastructure largely owned by one of the proponents.

## Claim 9: "World's most sustainable sovereign AI data centres"

**TELUS framing.** "Designed to be the world's most sustainable sovereign AI data centres, our Vancouver facilities will set a new global standard for sustainable AI infrastructure" (May 25, 2026).

**Response.** Sustainability superlatives need a comparator and a measurement. "World's most sustainable" is not a defined category. TELUS has not published a sustainability methodology, a third-party-verified PUE, a Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE) figure, embodied-carbon analysis for construction, or anchor-tenant disclosure that would let "sustainable AI" be evaluated. Until those are public and independently reviewed, the claim is marketing.

## Anticipated future framings to rebut

If TELUS publishes a standalone project page or runs a sustained communications campaign, the following are likely. Add rebuttals here as they appear:

- **"Net-zero by 20XX" pledges** paired with current carbon footprint silence.
- **"Canadian AI champion" framing** that conflates the proponent with the (still undisclosed) tenants.
- **"Industrial energy use is normal" framing** that elides the difference between a stable industrial load and a speculative AI load that may obsolete in 3–5 years as chips and models change.
- **"Better than the alternative" framing** (e.g., "if not us, US data centres") that treats demand as fixed rather than a policy choice.
