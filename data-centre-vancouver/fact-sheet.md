# TELUS's Vancouver AI Data Centre Cluster: What Vancouver Needs to Know

**Vancouver Centre NDP**
*Unceded xʷməθkʷəy̓əm, Skwxwú7mesh, and səlilwətaɬ territory | Data and sources reviewed and verified as of May 30, 2026.*

## What do they want to build?

On May 13, 2026, TELUS announced plans for a three-site AI data centre cluster in British Columbia, in partnership with Vancouver real estate firm **Westbank Corp.** and with federal support through Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada's *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres* program. TELUS is the **first publicly announced approved applicant** under that program. None of the three sites is built out to its announced configuration; the downtown Vancouver site is at the pre-application stage with no formal rezoning submitted to the City as of May 18, 2026.

The three proposed sites:

- **Kamloops — expansion of an existing facility.** A 215,000 sq ft data centre TELUS already operates; current 25 MW power draw and 12,500 GPUs, with planned capacity upgrades. Expansion online later in 2026.
- **Vancouver — Mount Pleasant (111 East 5th Ave).** A planned data centre, converting the M3 office building, which formerly housed part of Hootsuite's headquarters. Footprint figures in announcement materials vary (BetaKit reported 100,000 sq ft; Daily Hive reported 77,000 sq ft ultimate on-site). 13,000 GPUs, 26 MW at completion. Initial launch targeted late 2026, gradual scale-up through 2028.
- **Vancouver — Downtown (150 West Georgia / 720 Beatty St, beside BC Place).** A proposed 10-storey, 400,000 sq ft purpose-built facility, **100 MW power capacity, 50,000+ GPUs**, planned to sit atop an expanded Creative Energy district-energy steam plant. Operational target early 2029. **No formal rezoning application for the data centre use has been reported as submitted to the City.**

If built as announced, by 2032 the cluster would run more than **60,000 Nvidia GPUs** and draw over **150 MW**, with an initial 85 MW reported as secured from BC Hydro. TELUS has not disclosed the project cost. Claimed public benefits: roughly $9 billion in economic activity, 1,000 construction jobs, and 525 permanent positions across all three sites.

## Who is TELUS, and who is Westbank?

TELUS is one of Canada's three large telcos, headquartered in Vancouver. CEO **Darren Entwistle** announced the cluster on May 13, calling the new facilities "sexy as hell." The federal partner, AI Minister **Evan Solomon**, framed it as "the kind of ambitious infrastructure we need as a country."

Westbank Corp. is the Vancouver-based real estate developer best known for the Vancouver House tower, the Telus Garden complex, and Oakridge Park. Westbank is the developer partner on both Vancouver sites. The Mount Pleasant facility converts a Westbank-redeveloped office building; the downtown facility sits on a Westbank-controlled parcel beside BC Place, integrated with Creative Energy's expanded steam plant.

The federal program — *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres* — assessed 160 proposals on sovereignty, economic benefit, performance, energy, readiness, and cost. TELUS is the first project to clear the bar publicly. Specific federal contribution amounts have not been disclosed; Ottawa's role is described as a Memorandum of Understanding to follow current negotiations.

The *Tenant Explainer* later in this package picks up the trail of who will actually rent the compute.

## What are the concerns?

**No anchor tenants disclosed — sovereign for whom?** The "sovereign AI" branding is doing a lot of work in this announcement. TELUS has not named a single Canadian anchor tenant. The federal program's stated criteria include sovereignty and Canadian-law operation — but with 60,000 GPUs and 150 MW of capacity proposed to come online without disclosed tenants, the public has no way to verify that this infrastructure would primarily serve Canadian users. If, like the Bell Canada / CoreWeave / Cerebras arrangement in Regina, this capacity is leased to US-incorporated AI firms operating under US law, the *Sovereign* in the program name will mean nothing. The tenants will operate under the US CLOUD Act regardless of where the steel and concrete sit.

**A proposed 150 MW load on a grid still importing power.** BC Hydro's own 2025/26 second-quarter report shows net electricity imports of 2,041 GWh in the six months ending September 30, 2025 — down 56% from the drought-driven prior year, but still leaving the province dependent on the integrated Western Interconnection. Imports flow from Alberta and the US Pacific Northwest, where marginal generation remains coal- and gas-heavy. The 98%-renewable claim TELUS cites is accurate as a description of BC Hydro's *generation* mix on a typical year — but a marginal new 150 MW load is served by the marginal source, not the average. The BC cluster's projected annual demand (roughly 1.3 TWh at 150 MW running flat-out) is comparable in scale to what the province is currently net-importing. Adding hyperscale compute load to a grid in this position locks in import dependence and undermines BC's electrification of housing, transit, and industry.

**Water in a province in drought.** Metro Vancouver entered 2026 in continuing drought conditions, with reservoir levels and snowpack below the long-term norm; **Stage 2 water restrictions were in force as of late May 2026.** Data centres consume large volumes of water both directly (for cooling) and indirectly (through the thermal generation of the electricity they use). EarthJustice, the US public-interest law firm, estimates traditional data centres consume **one to five million gallons of water per day** each. TELUS has stated that the downtown 150 W Georgia facility will use a closed-loop liquid cooling system targeting **90% lower water consumption** than a traditional data centre, and in a May 25, 2026 statement to *Daily Hive* quantified this as **"300 million litres saved each year."** That figure should be read carefully: 300 ML/yr saved implies a traditional baseline well below what EarthJustice cites for a single hyperscale facility, and Metro Vancouver consumes roughly 1 billion litres of water per day — so the saving is less than a single day of regional consumption. The 90% claim, if independently verified, addresses one site partially. It does not cover the Mount Pleasant office conversion, the Kamloops expansion, or the indirect grid-water cost.

**Best use of downtown land?** Vancouver City Councillor **Sean Orr** has publicly asked whether data centres are the right use for limited urban infrastructure, energy, and space: *"If we were to ask the people of Vancouver how we should use our infrastructure, our energy, and our space, would they pick data centres or would they pick something else?"* Orr has also raised concern about whether costs for additional supporting infrastructure would fall on residents, and warned that data centres of this kind can be technologically obsolete within years. The Mount Pleasant site is immediately adjacent to residential neighbourhoods, raising local questions about noise, vibration, exhaust, and the conversion of office space.

**A federal program approving its first project before public debate has happened.** ISED's *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres* call closed and the first approval was announced inside a single news cycle. There has been no parliamentary committee review of the criteria, no public consultation on the program's design, and no published assessment matrix showing why this project cleared and others did not. Federal NDP Leader **Avi Lewis** has called for a pause on new data centre construction, saying "we can't sit back and let Big Tech billionaires decide our future."

**Local opposition is mobilizing.** On **May 23, 2026**, an estimated **750 people** marched from Waterfront Station through downtown Vancouver to City Hall against the cluster, organized by **No AI Data Centres in Vancouver** (`@no.ai.vancouver`). Speakers at City Hall included **BC Green Party Leader Emily Lowan**, **COPE Vancouver City Council candidate Solomon Yi-Kieran**, and march initiator **Torin LaRocque**. A second march is planned for **Saturday, June 27, 2026** at 1 p.m. The federal NDP petition and the broader movement are separate vehicles asking aligned questions of different levels of government.

## What about closed-loop cooling?

TELUS's central environmental claim — that the downtown facility will use closed-loop liquid cooling and consume 90% less water than a traditional data centre — is technically achievable and worth taking seriously. Closed-loop systems recirculate coolant rather than evaporating municipal water; they trade water consumption for additional electricity load to move heat through chillers and air-side rejection.

That trade is not free. More electricity means more grid draw, which in a deficit year means more imported gas and coal generation. A facility that wins on water and loses on carbon is not an environmental win — it is a substitution.

The Mount Pleasant office conversion has not been described in the same cooling-architecture detail. Neither has the Kamloops expansion. The cluster's environmental footprint cannot be assessed from the downtown site's headline number alone.

Global data centre water consumption is projected to rise sharply by 2030 in major International Energy Agency analyses; specific litre figures depend on which scenario is cited and are not reproduced here without direct reference to a current IEA publication. The general trend — rising water demand driven by AI compute — is well established.

## What is the NDP calling for?

NDP Leader Avi Lewis has committed to a [**Humans-First AI Policy**](https://lewisisleader.ca/ideas/dignified-work) that includes **pausing the expansion of AI data centres in Canada** — to protect clean water, to preserve grid capacity for human health and for the electrification of housing and transit, and to give our democratic institutions time to catch up with a technology that has been deployed without public debate.

For Vancouver Centre and the BC cluster specifically, that means:

- **Pause** the *Enabling Large-Scale Sovereign AI Data Centres* program and re-open it to parliamentary committee review.
- **Disclose** TELUS's anchor tenants before federal funds flow. If they are US-incorporated, the *Sovereign* framing has to be retired.
- **Independent water and grid assessment** of all three sites, with Metro Vancouver, BC Hydro, and First Nations rights-holders at the table.
- **Condition** any federal support on Canadian incorporation of the AI firms renting compute, on union construction labour, and on community benefit agreements with the host neighbourhoods.

The NDP draws a clear line between small-scale, publicly useful machine learning — medical research, public-service applications, accessibility — and the multi-billion-dollar corporate AI industry built on generative AI, mass data extraction, and the displacement of millions of workers.

---

## Who benefits? Who pays?

If built as announced, this cluster would benefit TELUS, Westbank, the still-unnamed AI companies that would rent the compute, and the federal department that gets to launch a flagship program with a Canadian-flag corporate face on it. The 150 MW of grid load, the cooling water, the construction disruption, the conversion of urban space, and the carbon cost of any imported generation needed to balance the load would stay with British Columbians.

525 permanent jobs across three sites is a real number. It is also a number that has to be set honestly against the scale of grid and water commitment being asked of a province already in drought and an energy deficit.

The petition this package supports asks the federal government to pause the expansion of AI data centres in Canada — starting with the TELUS BC cluster — until that conversation has happened on terms the community sets, not on a timeline the proponent does.
