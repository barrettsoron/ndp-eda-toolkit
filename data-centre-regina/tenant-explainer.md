# Who Are CoreWeave and Cerebras?

*CIA Money. MAGA Investors. Our Grid, Our Choice.*

**Regina–Wascana NDP**
*Background on the anchor tenants of Bell's proposed AI data centre in the RM of Sherwood.*

---

Bell Canada is building the facility. But the companies that will actually occupy it — the reason it exists — are **CoreWeave** and **Cerebras**, both American.

---

## CoreWeave: From Crypto Mining to AI Servers

CoreWeave was founded in 2017 as **Atlantic Crypto** by Michael Intrator, Brian Venturo, and Brannin McBee — three hedge fund traders who ran an Ethereum cryptocurrency mining operation. When the crypto market shifted, they pivoted the same GPU hardware to renting AI server capacity. The company rebranded to CoreWeave in 2019.

**Financial profile:** CoreWeave went public on the NASDAQ in March 2025. The IPO was troubled — they had to reduce both the offering price and size. The company is deeply unprofitable, carrying roughly **$7.5 billion in debt**, much of it collateralized against their Nvidia GPU contracts. A single customer — Microsoft — reportedly accounted for more than 60% of their revenue at the time of the IPO. If AI demand softens, the debt structure becomes precarious.

**Major investors:** Backers include **Magnetar Capital**, the hedge fund that became infamous for profiting from the 2008 financial crisis by betting against mortgage-backed securities it helped create. Other investors include Coatue Management, Jane Street, and Nvidia itself (which is both their supplier and an investor — a vertical entanglement that raises competition questions).

**Workforce:** Roughly 1,000–1,500 employees manage billions in infrastructure. This is a capital-intensive model that creates very few local jobs — consistent with the 80 permanent positions Bell is projecting for the Saskatchewan facility.

**Acceptable use:** CoreWeave has **no published policy** excluding surveillance, facial recognition, predictive policing, or military AI applications from its platform. Their business model is infrastructure-agnostic — they rent GPU capacity to any paying customer.

**US law exposure:** As a US-incorporated company, CoreWeave is subject to the **CLOUD Act**, which allows American law enforcement and intelligence agencies to compel access to data stored on their servers — regardless of where those servers are physically located. Data stored on CoreWeave infrastructure in Saskatchewan could be accessed by US authorities without Canadian judicial oversight.

## Cerebras: AI Chips for the Pentagon

Cerebras Systems builds wafer-scale AI processors — massive chips designed to train and run AI models. Their hardware is among the most powerful AI processors in the world. But the question isn't what the chips can do. It's who they're for.

**US military contracts:** Cerebras has **active, multi-phase contracts with DARPA** — the US Department of Defense's advanced research agency. It is currently in Phase 3 of the **Digital RF Battlespace Emulator** program, building real-time battlefield simulation and electronic warfare systems for the **US Navy**. In April 2025, DARPA selected Cerebras for an additional contract to deliver a "next generation, real-time compute platform for advanced military and commercial applications," with capabilities including real-time AI processing from military sensors and battlefield robotics.

**CIA funding:** Cerebras is a portfolio company of **In-Q-Tel** — the CIA's venture capital arm. In-Q-Tel describes Cerebras as "a cornerstone of In-Q-Tel's efforts to scale trustworthy, U.S.-based AI infrastructure for national security applications."

**Trump-linked investor:** In its $1.1-billion Series G funding round, Cerebras added **1789 Capital** as an investor. 1789 Capital is an anti-ESG venture fund co-founded by **Rebekah Mercer** — one of the largest donors to Donald Trump's political campaigns — with **Peter Thiel** instrumental in its creation. The fund explicitly aims to build an "America First" parallel economy.

**UAE and Chinese military ties:** Cerebras's largest customer, accounting for **87% of its revenue** in the first half of 2024, was **G42** — a UAE-based AI conglomerate. The US House Select Committee on China flagged G42's CEO for affiliations with "companies that develop dual-use technologies and materially support PRC military-civil fusion and human rights abuses." G42 had business ties to Huawei and BGI Genomics (a Chinese genomics firm placed on the US Entity List over surveillance concerns). Under pressure from the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS), G42 was forced to divest from Chinese companies, and its Cerebras stake was restructured to non-voting shares.

**Withdrawn IPO:** Cerebras filed for a public offering in 2024 but **withdrew it in October 2025** amid the CFIUS investigation into its G42 relationship. The company raised $1 billion privately and is reportedly planning a new listing.

## Every data centre is US jurisdiction

Both CoreWeave and Cerebras are subject to the **US CLOUD Act**, which compels American companies to hand over data to US authorities regardless of where it is physically stored. Data on servers in Saskatchewan is legally accessible to US intelligence and law enforcement without Canadian judicial oversight.

This is not an abstract legal concern. In 2025, when the International Criminal Court's chief prosecutor issued an arrest warrant for Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu, President Trump sanctioned the prosecutor and **Microsoft cut off his email access**. When the French Senate subsequently asked Microsoft France under oath whether it could deny US government requests for data stored within the European Union, the company admitted it **"cannot guarantee"** that.

As journalist Paris Marx has argued, every data centre operated by a US company functions as an extension of US jurisdiction — what European lawmakers now call a digital "kill switch." The Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement compounds this: it contains clauses that **limit Canada's ability to regulate cross-border data flows** or to require companies to store Canadian data within our borders.

Building a 300 MW facility for US military-intelligence-linked AI companies on Canadian soil isn't just an energy question or a jobs question. It is a sovereignty question.

## What this means for Saskatchewan

The $1.7-billion headline is Bell's number. But the facility is being built to serve these two companies — a debt-laden former cryptocurrency operation and a Pentagon AI contractor funded by the CIA and Trump-linked capital.

Saskatchewan is being asked to build a new natural gas pipeline, strain its electrical grid, and reshape public infrastructure for companies whose primary obligations are to American shareholders, American military agencies, and American law.

Eighty permanent jobs.
