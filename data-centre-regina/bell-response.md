## About this document

Bell has launched a public-facing page branding the Regina project as **"Bell AI Fabric"** with the tagline *"Sovereign AI infrastructure — built by Canadians, for Canada."* The page presents a curated set of answers on water, noise, power, and community impact, and closes with a generic "What can AI do for Canada" marketing section.

This document takes Bell's page claim-by-claim and supplies the context it leaves out. For the full evidence base, see `fact-sheet.md`, `tenant-explainer.md`, and the source list in this module's `README.md`.

---

## The framing: "Sovereign AI infrastructure — built by Canadians, for Canada"

This is the most important claim on the page, and the least supportable.

The 300 MW campus is being built to serve two anchor tenants — **CoreWeave** and **Cerebras** — both US-incorporated companies. Under the **US CLOUD Act**, American authorities can compel a US company to hand over data it controls, regardless of where that data is physically stored. Microsoft France told the French Senate under oath that it *"cannot guarantee"* it would refuse a US government data request for data sitting in European data centres. When President Trump sanctioned the International Criminal Court's chief prosecutor, Microsoft cut off his email. The legal regime running these servers is American — the steel and concrete sitting in Saskatchewan do not change that.

"Sovereign" is a marketing word. The tenancy, the regulatory exposure, and the ultimate control are not Canadian. See `fact-sheet.md` ("Canadian data in American hands") and `tenant-explainer.md` for the tenant backgrounder.

> **At the door:** *"Bell is the landlord. The tenants are American AI companies operating under US law. The jurisdiction running these servers is American — a Canadian address doesn't change that."*

## Economic claims

### "$12 billion in long-term economic value... including local employment and substantial municipal and provincial tax revenues."

The $12 billion figure includes the tenants' hardware — chips, servers, and networking gear manufactured elsewhere and leased to CoreWeave and Cerebras. It is not $12 billion landing in Saskatchewan paycheques or Saskatchewan supply chains. The operating profit on leased AI compute flows to Bell and to the US tenants, not to provincial coffers. See `fact-sheet.md` ("Eighty permanent jobs").

### "At least 800 construction jobs and 80 permanent operational roles... as many as 750 additional community jobs could result from a large data centre deployment of this nature."

Eighty permanent jobs for a 300 MW load on a provincial grid. The 750 "spin-off" figure is attributed to unspecified "industry research" — Bell does not name a study, a methodology, or a comparable facility. Comparable investment in energy-efficient buildings or renewable energy infrastructure generates 4 to 7 direct job-years of construction employment per million dollars — before multiplier effects — against an operational workforce of 80 staff for 300 MW of data centre load. See Bridge and Stanford, *Jobs for Today* (Centre for Civic Governance, 2025); `canvass-guide.md` ("Jobs").

### "Bell AI Fabric will strengthen productivity and competitiveness in Saskatchewan and Canada across key sectors including agriculture, energy, financial services, and critical infrastructure."

The announced tenants — CoreWeave and Cerebras — rent AI compute to their own customers on commercial terms. No commitment, tenant roster, or pricing arrangement ties capacity to Saskatchewan agriculture, energy, or financial-services users. A Saskatchewan farmer would access this facility on the same terms as a farmer in Ontario or Arizona.

## Water

Bell's page leads its environmental section with water, and on the narrow factual question it is mostly correct — the trade-off is what's missing.

### "Sealed closed-loop cooling system paired with air-cooled heat rejection... No evaporative cooling towers. No routine cooling-water demand. No draw on municipal water sources or groundwater."

Accept the claim: this design does not consume water the way evaporatively-cooled data centres do. That's a real difference from a facility in Arizona or Virginia.

**What the page does not say:** air-cooled heat rejection requires significantly more *electricity* than evaporative cooling to move the same heat. On a grid that runs on roughly 80–90% fossil fuels, that extra electricity translates directly to extra emissions. Bell has chosen to minimize a water footprint on a grid where water isn't the binding constraint — and to push the cost onto the grid, where the binding constraint is carbon. See `fact-sheet.md` ("What about water?") and `canvass-guide.md` ("Bell says they're using a closed-loop cooling system").

### "Technically, water is trucked in and added once during commissioning to fill the sealed system."

Also accept. Bell's page provides no numbers on cumulative trucked-in volume, or what happens when the system is drained, flushed, or topped up over its operating life. No independent environmental assessment exists.

## Energy and the grid

This is the section with the most important omissions.

### "Saskatchewan's grid, managed by SaskPower, includes a mix of natural gas, hydroelectric power and other sources."

**Coal is missing from this sentence.** Saskatchewan's grid is roughly 80–90% fossil fuels. At 630 g CO₂e per kilowatt-hour, its emission intensity is more than six times the national average of 100 g. Premier Scott Moe has committed to running coal until 2050 — two decades past the federal phase-out deadline. The province has said the data centre's baseload requirements help justify refurbishing aging coal plants. The "mix of natural gas, hydroelectric and other sources" phrasing is technically true only if "other sources" is doing an enormous amount of work. See `fact-sheet.md` ("A fossil-fuel anchor").

### "SaskPower will supply the power on a dedicated industrial feed, separate from residential distribution."

"Dedicated industrial feed" describes the routing, not the billing. Grid investments, transmission upgrades, and capacity reserves run through SaskPower's entire ratepayer base — the data centre's costs land in the same shared ledger as everyone else's.

### "Will electricity rates increase as a result of the additional load the Data Centre requires? No, rates are not going up as a result of the data centre being added to the grid."

This is the Virginia question. In Virginia — where data centres now cluster at industrial scale — regulators initially offered the same reassurance. They now project residential electricity bills could more than double by 2045 unless data centres pay their full share of grid costs. Dominion has already been granted rate increases. Virginia had to create a dedicated large-load rate class to try to shield households from further cost exposure — after the data centres were built, not before. The time to lock in household protection is before the load arrives, not after. See `fact-sheet.md` ("Energy costs for everyone") and `canvass-guide.md` ("Energy bills").

### Omission: the SaskEnergy gas pipeline

Bell's page does not mention that **SaskEnergy is building a new high-pressure natural gas pipeline and meter station** specifically to serve on-site gas generation at this facility. A publicly-owned gas utility is constructing dedicated fossil-fuel infrastructure for this project. That is a material fact about the "responsible energy use" framing on the page. See `fact-sheet.md` ("A fossil-fuel anchor").

### "Targeting a Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) of 1.3."

PUE measures facility overhead — how much of the total electricity goes to the servers versus cooling, lighting, and other building load. A PUE of 1.3 is reasonable for a modern facility. PUE tracks efficiency overhead, not total draw. A 300 MW load at PUE 1.3 is still a 300 MW load on a fossil-fuel grid.

## Community impact

### "Low-noise equipment... landscaped berms and setbacks... dark-sky-compliant lighting... careful drainage planning... acoustic modelling to guide final design and mitigation."

Every commitment on the page is phrased as design intent, not binding obligation. **No independent environmental assessment exists**, and no enforceable performance standards have been set for noise, light, drainage, or air emissions once the facility is operating. A covenant tying noise levels to enforceable numbers — something a landowner could actually sue over — does not exist.

Bell also held a private meeting with concerned landowners who had been formally requesting information for months. Surrounding residents still have no enforceable recourse if the facility is non-compliant — nothing on record and nothing binding. See `canvass-guide.md` ("Community impact").

### "Bell will fund project-specific infrastructure upgrades... not intended to shift costs onto local residents or the municipality."

"Project-specific" is narrower than "all costs." Bell is committing to roads and site access directly tied to construction. The long-run grid upgrades, gas pipeline costs, and emergency-response capacity that a 300 MW industrial facility pulls into the broader provincial ledger are outside that commitment.

## What Bell's page does not mention

A useful way to read the page is by what it leaves out. Bell's own public-facing information does not reference:

- **The tenants.** No mention of CoreWeave or Cerebras. The page never names the companies the facility is being built to serve.
- **US data sovereignty.** No mention of the CLOUD Act, CUSMA data-flow constraints, or the US legal regime that reaches the tenants.
- **The gas pipeline.** No mention of the dedicated SaskEnergy high-pressure gas pipeline and meter station.
- **Coal.** The "energy sources" answer omits coal from Saskatchewan's grid mix.
- **Treaty 4 and the George Gordon First Nation MOU.** The project sits on land held by GGFN, which has signed a partnership with Bell. Bell's own PR page does not centre that partnership. The Regina–Wascana NDP respects GGFN's decision to participate (see `fact-sheet.md` and `canvass-guide.md`) — the observation here is that Bell itself is not foregrounding it.

## A note on "What can AI do for Canada"

The page closes with a long marketing section on the benefits of AI: personalized healthcare, customized education, accelerated drug discovery, precision agriculture, and so on. None of this is tied to the Regina facility's actual tenants or their actual workloads. CoreWeave rents AI compute to third parties; Cerebras holds active DARPA contracts and CIA venture funding. Neither company has announced that capacity at this site will serve Saskatchewan hospitals, schools, or farms. This section of the page is generic AI-industry promotion, not a Regina-specific commitment.

At the door or at a table, the short version is:

> *"Bell's page is mostly about what this project isn't — it isn't using municipal water, the power feed isn't residential, the lights won't shine into your yard. Fine. The question is what it **is**: 300 megawatts of fossil-fuel-powered compute, leased to American AI companies under US law, built next to a Crown-owned gas pipeline SaskEnergy is building just to feed it. A pause gives us time to ask those questions before the concrete is poured."*
