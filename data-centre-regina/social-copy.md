## Longer (Facebook page post — 300–400 words)

**Title: "A $1.7-billion investment that leaves us holding the bill"**

Bell Canada is building Canada's largest purpose-built AI data centre campus on 160 acres in the RM of Sherwood, just south of Regina. The investment: $1.7 billion. The tenants: CoreWeave and Cerebras — two American AI companies. The permanent jobs: 80.

Let's be honest about what this means for Saskatchewan.

Our electrical grid runs on roughly 80% fossil fuels — coal and natural gas. At 630 grams of CO₂e per kilowatt-hour, Saskatchewan has one of the dirtiest grids in Canada. A 300 MW data centre on this grid isn't a step toward clean energy. It's a carbon anchor that locks us into fossil fuel dependence for decades.

SaskEnergy is already building a new high-pressure natural gas pipeline specifically to serve the facility. In Virginia, where data centres have clustered at industrial scale, regulators warn residential bills could more than double by 2045 without strict cost protections — and the state has already created a dedicated rate class to try to shield households. Saskatchewan families and businesses will bear the cost of the infrastructure this facility demands.

The governance process hasn't inspired confidence either. Four of seven RM of Sherwood council members resigned shortly after the announcement, and the province appointed interim replacements — meaning locally-elected oversight on a major infrastructure decision was replaced with provincial appointees.

And then there's data sovereignty. CoreWeave and Cerebras are US-incorporated companies. Under the American CLOUD Act, US authorities can compel access to data held by American companies — regardless of where it's physically stored. We're building critical AI infrastructure on Canadian soil for companies that answer to American law.

NDP Leader Avi Lewis has committed to a [Humans-First AI Policy](https://lewisisleader.ca/ideas/dignified-work) that includes **pausing the expansion of AI data centres in Canada** — to protect clean water, preserve affordable energy for human health and wellbeing, and give our democratic institutions time to catch up. The NDP supports useful applications of technology like medical research and clean energy management. What we're questioning is whether a 300 MW facility serving a former crypto mining company and a US military AI contractor should jump the queue ahead of community consent.

Bell already charges Canadians some of the highest phone and internet rates in the developed world. Now the same company wants our publicly-built SaskPower grid and a new publicly-funded SaskEnergy gas pipeline to sell cheap compute to American AI firms. SaskPower, SaskEnergy, and SaskTel were built by CCF and NDP governments as public goods — Saskatchewan invented the Crown corporation on the principle that some things must serve people first. And it's the same story across the economy: Loblaw posts record profits while families skip meals; Bell and Rogers charge some of the highest rates in the OECD; Cameco and Nutrien ship Saskatchewan's minerals to shareholders while communities absorb the cost. A public option — public grocery, real public telecom, public mineral royalties — is how working people claw back. A public grid that serves Saskatchewan families first, not American AI, is where we start.

We're collecting petition signatures in Regina–Wascana. If you think our energy, our water, and our democratic process should come first, add your name.

Message us or talk to a volunteer at your next local NDP event.

*— Regina–Wascana NDP*
